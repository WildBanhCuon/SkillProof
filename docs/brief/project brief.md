# **SkillProof — Project Brief**

**Priority persona:** HR / Talent Acquisition Lead (B2B buyer) **Scope:** User · Problem · Potential Solution (MVP: Junior Frontend Developer role)

## **1\. User — The HR / Talent Acquisition Lead**

**Profile.** HR managers, in-house recruiters, and tech hiring leads in companies actively recruiting junior tech profiles — typically scale-ups, mid-market tech companies, IT services firms, and digital agencies that hire 5–50 juniors per year. They own the screening funnel and are accountable for both the cost and the quality of junior hires.

**Why this persona is the priority.** HR is the buyer and the budget owner. They feel the pain in measurable, recurring ways — hours spent screening, cost-per-hire, time-to-fill, and the financial cost of a bad junior hire — which makes the value proposition concrete and the willingness to pay clear. The applicant side benefits, but it is HR who signs the contract, deploys the tool, and renews the subscription.

**Goals.** Hire qualified juniors faster, with less screening effort, with measurable confidence in the decision, and with fewer mis-hires — all while reducing dependence on subjective signals like school name or CV keywords.

## **2\. Problem**

Junior tech hiring is the most painful segment of the funnel for HR teams:

* **Application overload, low signal.** A single junior posting can attract 200–500+ applications. Most are off-target, but the recruiter still has to triage them. CVs at this level look interchangeable: same bootcamps, same buzzwords, same tutorial projects.  
* **CVs don't predict ability.** Without a professional track record, HR has no reliable way to tell a candidate who built a React app from one who watched a tutorial about React. The result is over-reliance on proxies — school reputation, prior internships — which is both unfair and a poor predictor of performance.  
* **Job ads are part of the problem.** Internal hiring managers often write postings that mix "junior" with senior-level requirements, which attracts the wrong candidates and discourages the right ones. HR sees the symptom (bad applications) without the lever to fix the cause (the ad itself).  
* **High cost of a bad junior hire.** A mis-hired junior costs 6–9 months of salary, onboarding, and management time before the company realizes the fit is wrong. Junior hiring is therefore both high-volume and high-risk — a bad combination.  
* **No defensible audit trail.** When a hire fails or a rejected candidate complains, HR has little structured evidence of why a decision was made. This is increasingly a legal and ESG concern.

The market gap: HR needs a way to **screen junior candidates on verified ability** at scale, without adding hours of manual evaluation.

## **3\. Potential Solution**

SkillProof is a B2B SaaS that plugs into the junior hiring workflow and turns it from a CV-sorting exercise into an evidence-based screening process. From the HR buyer's perspective, it delivers four concrete commercial benefits:

1. **Better job ads, fewer wasted applications.** The Job Ad Upgrade Studio analyzes each posting and flags unrealistic requirements, vague responsibilities, and missing information. Cleaner ads attract more relevant candidates and cut the application volume HR has to triage.  
2. **Automated skill verification.** Every applicant completes a short, role-calibrated test generated directly from the job ad. HR no longer relies on CV claims — they see scored evidence of what the candidate can actually do.  
3. **A verified shortlist, ready for interview.** The HR Dashboard ranks candidates by verified skill match, with a card-level view per candidate: scorecard, strengths, weaknesses, AI hiring recommendation ("ready now / trainable / not aligned"), and suggested interview questions. Screening time drops from hours to minutes.  
4. **A defensible, structured decision trail.** Every shortlist decision is backed by test evidence and a documented rubric — useful for internal review, candidate feedback, and DE\&I reporting.

**MVP success for HR.** A recruiter can take a vague junior frontend job ad, improve it in under 10 minutes, publish it with an auto-generated test, and receive a ranked shortlist of verified candidates — replacing what is currently a multi-day CV-screening exercise.

**Primary commercial metrics (the pitch to companies).**

* **Screening time reduction** per junior role (target: 60–70%)  
* **Interview-to-hire conversion rate** (target: \+30%)  
* **Reduction in mis-hires** measured at the 3–6 month mark  
* **Time-to-fill** for junior tech roles

**Business model signal.** Per-seat SaaS subscription for HR teams, with usage-based pricing tied to the number of roles published or candidates assessed. The applicant-facing experience (fair testing, useful feedback) is a quality and employer-brand differentiator that supports HR's recruiting and DE\&I goals — i.e., it is part of what HR is buying, not a separate product.

Out of scope for the MVP: real job marketplace, full ATS integration, live code-execution sandbox, multi-role test generation. The 4-week prototype focuses on a single role — Junior Frontend Developer — to demonstrate the full B2B value chain end-to-end: better ad → verified test → trusted shortlist.

# **Why this became possible *now***

## **1\. Transformers solved the scaling problem**

Before 2017, AI systems were mostly narrow:

* rule systems,  
* statistical models,  
* or small neural networks.

They failed at:

* long context,  
* reasoning across documents,  
* language flexibility,  
* generalized adaptation.

That is why one model can now:

* write,  
* summarize,  
* classify,  
* generate code,  
* evaluate candidates,  
* create interview questions,  
* analyze job ads,  
* and converse.

## **2\. Internet-scale data became available**

Earlier AI systems suffered from:

* insufficient labeled data,  
* narrow corpora,  
* expensive annotation.

Modern models train on:

* internet text,  
* code repositories,  
* documentation,  
* books,  
* enterprise data,  
* multimodal datasets.

The model learned latent structure from human behavior at scale.

This matters for your HR use case because:

* hiring language patterns,  
* frontend skill expectations,  
* competency frameworks,  
* job ad semantics,  
* and candidate behaviors

all exist in large quantities online now.

The model can infer:

* unrealistic requirements,  
* missing competencies,  
* contradictory expectations,  
* or weak screening signals

because it has statistically absorbed millions of examples.

## **The modern bottleneck is not information access**

It is:

* filtering,  
* prioritization,  
* verification,  
* synthesis,  
* and decision velocity.

In your HR example:

* 500 applicants,  
* weak CV signals,  
* inconsistent screening,  
* legal risk,  
* time pressure.

Humans cannot economically process that volume well.

## **The cost structure changed**

Historically:

* human judgment was cheaper than automation.

Now in many workflows:

* AI inference is cheaper than junior analyst time.

# **Why these problems stayed unsolved for so long**

Because the problems were not actually “software problems.”

They were:

* ambiguity problems,  
* context problems,  
* judgment problems.

Traditional software is deterministic.

Hiring is not deterministic.
